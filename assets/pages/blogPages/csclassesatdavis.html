CS Classes at Davis

Hey there!

Here, I'll be sharing with you what computer science classes I've taken at UC Davis and my thoughts on them. If you don't go/plan on going to UC Davis, this may not make too much sense to you. Classes will be listed in the order in which I took them.

DISCLAIMER!! Some of the following course information assumes that you are going to major in CSE (Computer Science and Engineering) and not CS.

ECS 30

(Programming and Problem Solving)

Professor: Matt Bishop
Required: Yes
Difficulty: 4/10
Pre-requisites: MAT 21A (may be concurrent)
Skills/Concepts: C, Pointers, Linked Lists, Stacks, Vim.
Taken: Year 1, Fall

ECS 30, my very first programming course. Actually, I took one in High School, but it was more of an experimental course. In other words, there wasn't many concepts I learned from that high school course that I took to college.

That being said, UC Davis suggests taking ECS 10 first if you are completely new to programming. I am here to suggest that you will be fine starting with ECS 30 as your first course. Yes, taking ECS 30 as your first course may require a bit more work out of you, but it's way better than spending a quarter taking a class that "warms" you up to the rigor computer science courses have to offer. After all, you will be up against time for the rest of your life, so there's no reason to waste it taking classes you don't need.

The actual work that this course required was not high in quantity at all. The department knows that this is the course many computer science students take first, so their plan is not to burn you out in your first course. None of the programs (which was about 1 a week, some with multiple parts) were terrible long/difficult to understand.

In short, take this class ASAP. If you are new to programming, it will require a bit of work to learn all the material thoroughly, but it's completely do-able.

ECS 40

(Software & Object Oriented Programming)

Professor: Sean Davis
Required: Yes
Difficulty: 8/10
Pre-requisites: ECS30
Skills/Concepts: C/C++, UNIX Command Line, Shell Scripting, Classes, Pointers, Inheritance, Polymorphism, Stacks, Queues, Vectors, Maps/Multimaps.
Taken: Year 1, Winter

If you are new to programming in general, this class will be a struggle. Sean does not have you writing those simple "calculator" programs that you'll find in a lot of synonymous classes (sorry, but I find those programs simple as hell, with minimal value). He will assign programs that have been refined over the years to pose as a manageable challenge to the student. With these programs being assigned once a week, you're in for a challenging course.

But don't be afraid. In this class, you will learn a lot. In addition to learning how to program with C/C++, you'll learn how to use the command line. The command line is another discussion in itself, but it is an extremely beneficial skill that every computer scientist should have, to say the least. Unfortunately this information may not be very useful as Sean is retiring very soon. But if you do find this and see that Sean is teaching a course you have to take, do take. It'll be worth your while. Additionally, a lot of his lectures are archived, so you can view them at 1.5x at home!

ECS 20

(Discrete Math for CS)

Professor: Patrice Koehl
Required: Yes
Difficulty: 7/10
Pre-requisites: MAT 21A
Skills/Concepts: Truth tables, proof by induction, proof by contradiction, sets, functions, Big-O notation, recursion/solutions of recurrence relations
Taken: Year 1, Spring

This class will also require some work. For me, I couldn't just go to lecture and Ace the exams without supplemental practice. Unfortunately, I didn't get this idea nailed in to me until the final came around.

That being said, my tips for this class would be to:

  1. Go to lecture
  2. Go to discussion
  3. Do practice exams/problems from discussions/from his website

Professor Koehl is an extremely caring professor. He wants all of his students to do well, so he provides many resources for you to do so. So, take advantage of this. There's not many professors who have such a well established learning environment, so make use of it. Doing extra problems that can be found on his website will greatly help you when the exams come around. It would be in your best interest to do them.

ECS 50

(Machine Dependent Programming)

Professor: Matthew Butner
Required: Yes
Difficulty: 3/10
Pre-requisites: ECS40
Skills/Concepts: How data is represented in a computer, Intel X86, assembly, hardware-software interaction, caching, virtual memory.
Taken: Year 1, Spring

Here's some pretty data!


From left to right, those are the grade distributions for midterm 1, midterm 2, and the final exam. With the slight exception of midterm 2, there is a heavy skew to the left. Hopefully, it goes without saying that this class is very manageable. What makes the average on the exams so high? Well, it's a combination of allowing open notes and test recycling. I don't remember what happened to the averages on midterm 2 with the class, but the exams are generally pretty easy. Generally, the exams will require you to write machine code and recite facts discussed in lecture.

As far as class content, a majority of what you'll be learning revolves around assembly language. I admit it's not the most enjoyable language. It's one of those languages that eats up a lot of your time with not much knowledge gain in return. It's a pretty simple language to grasp, just not too interesting.


ECS 60

(Data Structures and Programming)

Professor: Sean Davis
Required: Yes
Difficulty: 7/10
Pre-requisites: ECS40
Skills/Concepts: Trees, graphs/graph algorithms, sorting algorithms, big O
Taken: Year 2, Fall

By the time you're reading this, Sean is probably retired. Regardless of that fact, I'll still share my thoughts about the class with him.

Sean is pretty much the only prof/lecturer to regularly teach ECS60 here at Davis. There's a couple other professors, but they're usually found teching it in the summer. This class is pretty much the most important class to take just because it opens up many doors to upper division courses and career opportunities. Do note that when I say it open doors to career opportunities, it DOES NOT mean you can just walk out of the class with an A and expect to know everything to pass a technical interview at Google. The required knowledge for such a task is another topic in itself.

In this class, you'll learn about a bunch of data structures/trees including Splay Tree, BST, AVL Tree, B+ Tree, Hash Tables, Heaps, and a couple other I'm probably forgetting. You'll also learn about algorithms including Dijkstra's, Ford-Fulkerson, Prim's, Kruskal's, Topological sort, DFS, BFS, Articulation Points, Huffman Encoding, Smart Unions, and, again, probably a couple more I'm forgetting. The sorting algorithms you learn include insertion sort, quick sort, radix sort, shell sort, heap sort, merge sort, indirect sort, bucket sort, and external sort. (Note to self: Organize this list into a prettier format)

All of the above algorithms are pretty easy to understand/perform. Most of Sean's tests will ask you to perform a select few algorithms and they're generally pretty straight forward. The majority of the difficulty that comes from the class is with his challenge programs (2). These challenge programs will more than likely require you to implement one of the listed algorithms/data structures to perform a task that Sean has created. I should note that the solutions to his challenge programs are sometimes the most simple data structure, so don't over think them. Be sure to start early to allow time for debugging too!
